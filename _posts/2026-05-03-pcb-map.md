I used a free PCB sponsorship as an excuse to build the biggest board I’ve ever designed. I turned it into a living map of my family.

To my great surprise, [PCBWay](https://www.pcbway.com/) reached out and offered to cover the manufacturing costs for a board in exchange for a mention on this hallowed blog. While I've avoided monetizing this blog in any way ([including removing analytics]({% post_url 2022-12-19-server-side-site-analytics %})), it turns out I’m not incorruptible when presented with an excuse to design a giant PCB. As you’ll probably be able to tell from the rest of the article, PCBWay had no involvement itself in the project or write up beyond agreeing to manufacture it.

I like using constraints as inspiration, and I wanted to build something that took advantage of the PCB medium itself.

[<img class="center" src="{{ site.image_host }}/2026/map_pcb/real_map.webp">]({{ site.image_host }}/2026/map_pcb/real_map.jpg)

All of the designs and source code for this project can be found at: [https://github.com/axlan/pcb-map](https://github.com/axlan/pcb-map)

While I don’t think I rushed the project, I did try to move faster than usual. With a new baby in the house, project time could sometimes feel borrowed, either from my family or from my already limited sleep. Still, making things is important to me, and in its own way, this project became a form of self-care. In some places that tradeoff worked out well. In others, not so much. See the [Improvements](#improvements) section for the consequences of moving a little too quickly.

More recently, I made a [little game handheld]({% post_url 2021-10-29-custom-pcb %}) that could play snake or track the state of a Magic the Gathering game. Here, I had fun adding some decorative flourishes and got to practicing using a modern KiCad version.

# Brainstorming

While I knew I wanted the PCB itself to be the centerpiece, I went through a long list of ideas before settling on one.

I spent some time specifically looking at other PCB art projects. There's a lot of [beautiful](https://hackaday.com/tag/pcb-art/) [stuff](https://hackaday.com/tag/badge/) [out](https://boldport.com/shop/dreamer) [there](https://hackaday.com/2016/01/28/beautiful-and-bizarre-boards/). One site I found particularly interesting was [Boldport](https://boldport.com/). In particular,  <https://boldport.com/blog/2013/09/the-lifegame.html> reminded of my [recent wedding project]({% post_url 2025-10-02-eye-chart-hidden-message %}). They actually have a whole design tool for PCB art 
<https://github.com/boldport/pcbmode> which I looked into. Unfortunately, it seems like the site and the tool haven't really been active for a few years.

Another PCB art project that inspired me was <https://chaijiaxun.com/blog/pcb-metro-map-build-log/>. The author created meticulous transit maps as PCBs with light animations.

<img class="center" src="{{ site.image_host }}/2026/map_pcb/95dd423edd5fa2da682620668c5b896e_original.webp">

These two projects both dealt with illuminating PCB's from the back. I used them as points of reference as I settled on my own design. The creator of the [PCB Metro Map](https://chaijiaxun.com/blog/pcb-metro-map-build-log/) Chai Jia Xun in particular spent a lot of effort on perfecting his approach. He ended up shining light through the via holes in the board, coloring them with paper, and using a printed mask to avoid light leaking between holes.

With my prior art research done, I focused on coming up with my own design.

My default instinct for an interesting display project is always to turn it into a clock. I explored designs that combined static PCB artwork with dynamic elements like LEDs, LCDs, e-ink panels, and seven-segment displays. I considered building a traditional clock, a calendar, and even an [orrery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orrery). But the more I explored those ideas, the more conventional they felt. I wanted something stranger and more personal.

In the end, the most interesting direction was a display that reflected the daily rhythms of my family in real time.

Of course, this takes inspiration from the "Weasley family clock" from Harry potter. Many people have made beautiful projects recreating the clock, and a few that incorporate the electronics to make it function like in the books as well. To put my own spin on it, I decided to combine the idea with the [PCB Metro Map](https://chaijiaxun.com/blog/pcb-metro-map-build-log/) to make the display a map.

# High Level Design

First, I had to settle a few basic layout questions:

 - What geographic area should the map cover?
 - Should the map be contiguous, or split into multiple regions?
 - Should it include zoomed-in sections?
 - How would I indicate which family member was which? Would I need RGB LEDs to create distinct colors?

I also considered an approach where a row of circles along the bottom of the board would call out points of interest like home or school, with lines connecting them back to their locations on the map.

There was also the question of how to light the board. I could either place LEDs on top of the PCB or mount them underneath it. LEDs mounted below the board would need some way for the light to shine through. Either holes through the PCB or regions without copper. The [PCB Metro Map project](https://chaijiaxun.com/blog/pcb-metro-map-build-log/) meticulously tested the lighting and tempted me to take one of the more complicated approaches. Chai Jia Xun ended up shining light through via holes in the PCB, coloring them with paper, and using a printed mask to prevent light from leaking between holes.

As I explored lighting options, I also started thinking about the assembly process. I’ve built several projects that required soldering dozens of RGB LEDs (like the [Icosahedron Globe]({% post_url 2020-06-21-icosahedron-travel-globe %}) and the [Pixel Wreath]({% post_url 2017-07-04-wreath-pixel-display %})) and it can be both tedious and finicky. Over time, I’ve learned that starting with the right off-the-shelf LED hardware can save an enormous amount of effort.

Since the goal was ultimately to illuminate points on a map, what I really needed was a dense LED grid. It turns out there are plenty of inexpensive RGB LED matrix panels available, and I found 64x32 panels selling for around $30. That was dramatically cheaper (and far less work) than populating the board with individual RGB LEDs.

Once I committed to using an LED matrix, the rest of the design started to come together. I decided to make the largest PCB I could reasonably pair with a 64x32 panel. I included the surrounding hills and water to create a more balanced, square composition, while centering the LED panel on the areas we actually travel through.

After ordering a panel, I found that it looked fine shining through a test PCB without copper or solder mask, which simplified the design considerably.

As usual, I ended up using an ESP32 development board to drive the display.

# Making the PCB

## PCB Design

All the design files can be found at <https://github.com/axlan/pcb-map/tree/master/design>.

I usually try to make my projects as automated and repeatable as possible (often to a fault). I’ll spend far more time building tooling and infrastructure than it would take to do a one-off task manually. For the map artwork, though, I gave myself permission to just wing it and focus on getting the design finished as directly as possible.

I initially considered using the [Boldport PCBmodE tool](https://github.com/boldport/pcbmode), but it appeared to be unmaintained and seemed like it would come with a steep learning curve. Instead, I decided to try the image converter now built into KiCad.

Since the KiCad converter only works with bitmap images (JPGs, PNGs, and similar formats), I didn’t need to worry about building everything as vector artwork.

I also explored more abstract map styles, including this design from [Archie’s Press](https://archiespress.com/collections/city-map-prints-minimalist-neighborhood-map-art-archies-press/products/bay-area-map):

[<img class="center" src="{{ site.image_host }}/2026/map_pcb/BayArea_1024x1024@2x.webp">](https://archiespress.com/collections/city-map-prints-minimalist-neighborhood-map-art-archies-press/products/bay-area-map)

In the end, though, I decided to stick with a normal geographic map.

I was surprised by how difficult it was to find a good source for the map imagery. If I had wanted to be truly meticulous, there are probably mapping APIs that expose the underlying geographic features directly so you can render everything exactly how you want. Instead, I mainly needed an online map tool where labels (road names, points of interest, etc.) could be disabled cleanly.

Eventually, I found [Snazzy Maps](https://snazzymaps.com), which was almost exactly what I was looking for. It provides a JSON-based styling interface with fairly fine-grained control over map appearance. I started from the existing [Assassin’s Creed IV style](https://snazzymaps.com/style/72543/assassins-creed-iv) and disabled railroads and local streets.

The site includes an export button, but it only supports images up to 1000x1000 pixels. Instead, I rendered the map at high resolution and captured screenshots manually.

Next I had to decide what map features I wanted to have on which PCB layers.

<img class="center" src="{{ site.image_host }}/2026/map_pcb/0_NiplN4TgWC6sJxPz.webp">

PCBs normally have these layers on both the front and back of the board, though additional copper layers can also be sandwiched internally. Since I planned to shine light through the PCB, I realized I could also use the back-side layers to block or dim light without affecting the visible front surface. Vias (the plated holes that connect copper layers together) could even be used to channel light directly through the board.

In the end, I decided to keep things simple:

 - Solder mask for water
 - Exposed copper for streets
 - Bare PCB substrate for land

To make the board translucent, I removed the copper and solder mask from the entire back side of the PCB. Once I started adding electronics, though, I limited this treatment to the land areas so that I could still place conventional components in the "water" regions.

At that point, I just needed to generate images for each PCB layer I wanted to import into KiCad. Here was the process I ended up using:

1. Using [Snazzy Maps](https://snazzymaps.com), I captured a screenshot of the region I wanted to use for the map.
2. Since I had decided on a final board size of 240x320 mm, I cropped the image to match the correct aspect ratio.
3. I created a reference layer with a 160x320 mm rectangle to verify that the LED matrix panel would fully cover the parts of the map I wanted illuminated.
4. Using [GIMP](https://www.gimp.org/), I used the "select by color" tool to separate the image into independent layers for water, land+roads, and roads. I made each layer a solid opaque color.
5. The road layer ended up looking heavily pixelated and didn’t import cleanly into the KiCad image converter. To fix this, I copied the roads image into [Inkscape](https://inkscape.org/) and used the "trace bitmap" feature to generate a smoothed vector version of the roads. I then re-exported this at a higher resolution and brought it back into GIMP to create cleaner road geometry.
6. I exported PNG images for both the land+roads layer and the roads layer. I also created a horizontally flipped version of the land+roads image for use on the back side of the PCB.
7. Using the KiCad image converter, I imported the PNGs into the PCB design at a final scale of 240x320 mm:

   1. The roads became a top-side copper layer.
   2. The land+roads image became a top-side solder mask layer.
   3. The flipped land+roads image became a bottom-side solder mask layer.

At that point, I had a PCB file containing the complete map artwork.

Separately, I created another KiCad project for the actual electronics: the ESP32 development board, LED matrix interface, and power connectors. After verifying the footprints and laying out the PCB connections, I copied the completed electronics layout into the map PCB project.

At one point I considered trying to keep the board single-sided, so I intentionally selected ESP32 GPIO pins that minimized routing complexity and avoided needing additional copper layers.

When I finally ran the design rule checker (DRC), I discovered a few clearance violations near the board edges, so I added roughly half a millimeter of additional margin around the design. It also had issues with the single sided board, so I left it two sided.

One feature I especially appreciated during this process was KiCad’s 3D PCB viewer, which made it easy to preview how the finished board would actually look before sending it off for manufacturing.

## PCB Manufacturing

I was curious how [PCBWay](https://www.pcbway.com/) would feel about my large art board, so I was pretty eager to get it into their system and see what would happen.

There UI for submitting a design was easy enough. As someone who has a bit of experience, but is no professional I found it a fairly painless process. I played around with the options and was briefly tempted to get a surface finish of "hard gold". This would have plated the copious exposed copper on my board with solid gold. A 30 micron thick layer of gold jumped the price estimate up from from $120 to $572. I figured the normal silver finish would probably look better and be less likely to be rejected.

It was pretty nice I could see a preview of the board after I uploaded it, to double check my choices of mask color and finish matched the aesthetics I was going for.

With the board submitted I had to wait for a design review. I was curious how this would go, and sure enough my design was flagged:

> Hi,
> 
> Thanks for your inquiry, here is a question for project No.W1047802AS4C1_map_board.zip
> 
> Pls find the attached pic and confirm whether the design is normal or not?
> 
> Your timely response would be greatly appreciated!

The pic was the entire copper layer of my board.

I responded with:

> This is the intended layout for the top copper layer. The large section is there for appearances only to make the board look like a metal map.
> Thanks for double checking.

which got the reply:

> Thanks for your reply!
>  W1047802AS4C1_map_board has been approved now, the PCB price is $126.17, with an estimated lead time of 3-4 days, pls kindly login to check the details and proceed to checkout if everything is in order. :D 

Honestly, that's about the right interaction. It was certainly worth them double checking that a board that was mostly "non-functional" was the intended design, but it was easy enough to clarify. I'm curious what the back and forth would have looked like if I hadn't cleaned up the normal design rule checks before I submitted it.

From there everything went smoothly. They covered the cost of production and shipping, though I needed to deal with the tariffs. For that Fedex just sent an invoice after completing the delivery with 31% tariff the plus a $15 dollar fee for the prepaying during the import process. 

# Software

From the start, I knew I'd want to keep the firmware on the panel as simple and generic as possible. Ideally, I could reuse this firmware for any other panel display projects I might have in the future. This interface would get commands over MQTT to drive the displays dynamic behavior.

This meant I'd also need to write the control software that would run on a host PC to handle querying the families location and sending the commands to the device.

## LED Matrix Driver

I was bought a LED matrix driver with the hope that controlling it wouldn't be too much of a pain. From what I can tell, the HUB75 is an informal standard that many LED panels implement <https://learn.lushaylabs.com/led-panel-hub75/>. Since it's informal, there's many small variations that need to be validated when buying a new model.

The matrix display that this project was tested with is the:
[Waveshare RGB Full-Color LED Matrix Panel, 5mm Pitch, 64x32 Pixels](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0CLV5MHPX)

It was labeled as `P5(2121)-3264-16S-M5` (32x64 1/16 scan) and used the `RUC7258D`, `SM16208SJ`, and `MW245BC` control chips.

Like most embedded projects, finding the "best" library to use is a bit of a pain. It seems many owe their ancestry to the Adafruit graphics library which has many defendants targeting different platforms and display technologies. After some research, I decided that the <https://github.com/mrcodetastic/ESP32-HUB75-MatrixPanel-DMA> library seemed to be the most capable and compatible for the ESP32.  

I wired up the panel straight to an ESP32 and tried the example script. The display that sort of worked, but everything was slightly glitchy or shifted over. Reading the README, I tried changing the clock phase setting, and just like that the panel was working correctly.

I did a bit more experimenting and eventually did find other display artifacts, but these went away when I switched from the jumper cables, to my custom PCB. As it turns out, they're not kidding when they say these boards are sensitive to needing a beefy power supply and short cables. The signal cable issues might also be partly due to the panel expecting 5V signals while the ESP32 uses 3.3V.

When my custom PCB came, I was amazed and delighted that the connection between the ESP32 and the LED Panel worked without issue. It's not that it's particularly complicated, but there's a lot of room for something to get flipped right/left or top/button when I was doing the layout.

As I developed my application I found the [panel library](https://github.com/mrcodetastic/ESP32-HUB75-MatrixPanel-DMA) mostly worked how I'd expect and was petty solid. When I started having issues with flickering I enabled the libraries built in double buffering which fixed the issue.

With the driver done, I needed to implement the display control interface. I didn't do this particularly systematically, and mostly just added features as needed. I ended up with two basic ways to draw things on the display.

First, I added an interface for drawing a background image. This is just sending a bitmap of the pixel colors for the whole panel to be statically displayed if nothing else over writes them. When I started testing with actually sending this, I realized that the MQTT library I was using had a 256 byte message limit. Since I didn't want to rely on sending 4KB (64x32x2 (2 is the two bytes per pixel for the RGB565 color scheme)) messages, I set up the interface to be able to update the background row by row (128 byte payloads).

I also wanted to support drawing elements that could be updated independently and could have some dynamic behaviors (e.x. pulsing). I created a concept of sprites that were indexed with a name identifier. The only kind of sprite that I added was a pixel that could oscillate between two colors. This also enabled the panel to periodically switch which sprite was being shown in the case that two had the same position. This was to support the case when multiple people were at the same address and I wanted both to show up on the map. These sprites would then be updated on future commands that referenced their name. There are some other sprites like driving routes I could have added, but decided to not add complexity to the firmware until I needed to.

## Network Discover Provisioning and Configuration

One of the first things I focused on when building out the firmware was making the network configuration completely reliable. In previous projects, I’d taken a variety of approaches to handling Wi-Fi credentials and MQTT configuration, but this time I wanted something more polished and dependable. I put extra effort into making the setup process stable and user-friendly so that, if I ever wanted to duplicate the project or revisit it in the future, getting it running again would be straightforward.

Setting or updating the Wi-Fi network credentials requires a side channel to configure the device before it has network access. For the first time, I read through the [Espressif provisioning documentation](https://docs.espressif.com/projects/esp-idf/en/stable/esp32/api-reference/provisioning/index.html). The [Espressif unified provisioning libraries](https://docs.espressif.com/projects/esp-idf/en/stable/esp32/api-reference/provisioning/provisioning.html) provide a robust way to provision the device over Bluetooth or an ad-hoc Wi-Fi access point. The downside was that the library is fairly complex and requires the host machine to either support Bluetooth or manually connect to the ad-hoc Wi-Fi network.

There was also a library called [SmartConfig](https://docs.espressif.com/projects/esp-idf/en/stable/esp32/api-reference/network/esp_smartconfig.html), which uses some clever techniques to monitor Wi-Fi traffic and decode network credentials from the length of encrypted packets. Unfortunately, the documentation suggested that this approach may not work consistently across generic Wi-Fi drivers.

I ultimately fell back on the [WiFiManager library](https://github.com/tzapu/WiFiManager), which I’ve used in previous projects. It still requires manually connecting to an ad-hoc Wi-Fi network, but it creates a captive portal that makes entering credentials very straightforward.

To streamline the process of finding the device on the network, I spent some time better understanding how mDNS works. At least on my Linux system, it allowed me to use the device’s local hostname directly instead of needing to determine its assigned IP address.

Once the device is connected and configured, all communication happens over MQTT. This gives me flexibility in choosing between a local or remote broker, handling authentication, and avoiding a lot of lower-level protocol edge cases. However, even after the device was online, I still needed a way to configure the MQTT host and authentication settings.

I ended up creating a very simple UDP command packet format that could configure all of the parameters used for MQTT authentication. While I spent a significant amount of time understanding MQTT TLS certificate management, I ultimately disabled certificate validation.

To support arbitrary brokers securely, I would also have needed to transmit the root certificate through this configuration interface. The added complexity of managing certificates simply did not feel worth the relatively small security benefit of mitigating potential man-in-the-middle attacks for this project. If I were building a commercial device, the situation would be different: I would likely target a specific broker, embed the root certificate directly in the firmware, and avoid needing this level of configurability altogether.

I'll go into the details of how I tried to automate the setup for the device in the Python controls [find-devices](#find-devices) section.

## Simulator

While updating the firmware and testing on the device isn't that painful, I wanted to make development as quick as possible. Since the interface to the display is so basic, it was relatively easy to create a small CMake application to simulate it locally.

I primarily wanted to test the MQTT handling and display logic, so I included the relevant files from the firmware in my simulator build. To do this I created dummy header files that mocked out any Arduino or driver calls to allow them to compile. I used the SFML graphics library to draw my simulated panel overlaid a transparent map picture.

## Python Controls

I made two python tools for interacting with the board. I continue to mostly design Python projects with uv in mind, so here I really tried to get good command line ergonomics. I used the Typer library for the first time to handle parsing the command line arguments. It ended up working out pretty well once I learned enough to cover my use cases. One feature I was happy to find, was that you can map the command line arguments to environment variables. This gives an easy way to set credentials without needing to type them in each time without needing to add extra code.

### device-setup

The goal of this tool was to eliminate the annoying process of setting up an IoT device where you need to go through the router to find it on the network, or not know if it's still not connecting to the WiFi. The states it detects and configures are:

### control-server

Once the device is fully configured, further operation is controlled over MQTT. This tool handles that control for various tests and applications.

The main application is `display-shared-locations`. It loads the user and their friends' positions to draw on the map. It also checks calendar events for positions to draw routes to.

For testing purposes I also created a way to specify the changing user positions and routes as a JSON file.

One issue I hit in developing this interface, is that there's not a great way to share your location over an API from Google. Google has your position and let's you share it with friends over Google Maps, but there's not an official way to access this data besides through the app or website. While there is a Python library that handles parsing the data, you need to provide with session cookies which can be rather fragile for a long running server.

I could use a side channel to report positions, but I wanted to avoid needing my family to run any special on their phones. This is both for simplicity and for security.

I could get position data from other services as well. At some point if my kids have some sort of non-phone location beacon (like an Airtag) I could potentially integrate that in as well since it's jus tan update to the Python server.

Drawing the routes was a bit more complicated. I initially used an API from OpenRouteService since its free tier would have covered my usage. However, it gave incorrect positions for the test addresses I tried. I switched to using the Google Maps routing API which requires a Google Cloud account, but should also effectively be free.

While drawing the locations of the users on the map was a fairly trivial conversion between the latitude/longitude and the panels X/Y, drawing the routes was a bit more complicated. I get the route as a series of lines, and I needed to map these onto the grid. This is something I could almost certainly improve, but got something quick and dirty working.

Actually drawing the routes on the map was something I decided to add later in the process. I could have added a new type of sprite for handling the routes, but I decided it would be easier to draw them as part of the background image and manage them from the server side.

# Improvements

## Hardware
1. Choose a more common ESP32/ESP8266 footprint - The particular board I had on hand was one I got from AliExpress years ago. It seems like the pinout it used wasn't very standardized. Since I'm using it on a printed board, it probably would have been worth targeting a more well documented dev board pinout.
2. Select a thinner PCB - One of the PCBWay options I left at the default was the board thickness. While the lights are visible through the PCB, they are a little dimmer in daylight then I'd like. I think this would have been simply addresses by ordering a thinner board.
3. Avoid blocking ESP32 antenna - I laid out the connectors for the board without much thought. If I had thought about it, I probably wouldn't have put the ESP32 on the back behind a solid layer of copper. While it still works, the WiFi signal strength is much worse then it could be, and probably wouldn't work at all if it wasn't fairly near the router. 
4. Avoid blocking the ESP32 USB - A similar mistake was putting the ESP32 very close the the LED matrix HUB75 connector.
